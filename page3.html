Estimated emissions by method

Tonnes CO₂ / CO₂e for this single voyage, under each methodology.

Methods 1 (IMO–AER) allocates emissions based on the ship's full carrying capacity, regardless of how much of it your cargo actually used. Methods 2–4 allocate only the share of emissions corresponding to the cargo actually carried.

When a shipment uses a small share of a tanker's total capacity, capacity-based allocation (AER) can substantially overestimate the emissions attributable to that cargo — the ship visual above shows why. Cargo-based methods (EEOI, GLEC TTW, GLEC WTW) are generally a closer match to actual attributable emissions, though they assume the rest of the ship's capacity was used by other cargo.

TTW (tank-to-wake) covers only fuel combustion onboard. WTW (well-to-wake) adds the emissions from producing and transporting that fuel to the ship — which is why WTW is typically the more comprehensive, and often recommended, figure.

Emission-intensity factors (AER, EEOI, GLEC TTW, GLEC WTW) for chemical/product tankers in the 10,000–19,999 DWT range are illustrative reference values drawn from the published IMO Fourth IMO GHG Study (2020) and the GLEC Framework v3.1 — both publicly available methodologies, not specific to any organization. I haven't re-verified these against the current published tables myself; if you're using this for actual reporting, check the latest editions before relying on the figures. All voyage inputs above are illustrative and don't represent a real shipment.
